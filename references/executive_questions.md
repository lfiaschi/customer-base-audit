# Executive Questions Reference

Key questions executives should be able to answer after a customer-base audit, organized by lens/chapter. Derived from "The Customer-Base Audit" by Fader, Hardie & Ross.

---

## Foundational Questions (Chapters 1-2)

These questions establish the prerequisites for a meaningful audit.

1. **How does your company define a customer?** What criteria underlie the definition, and is there alignment across business units and functional areas?
2. **How many unique customers do you have?** Not transactions, not visits, not accounts -- unique individuals or entities.
3. **Can you produce a customer x time dataset?** Can every transaction be tied to a specific customer, with date, spend, and ideally profit?
4. **What proportion of your transactions are associated with known customers?** How much of your revenue is invisible at the customer level?
5. **How do you allocate costs to each customer?** Can you compute customer-level profit, even if only spend minus direct product costs?

## Lens 1: Customer Heterogeneity (Chapter 3)

*How different are your customers?*

1. **How concentrated is your customer base?** What fraction of customers accounts for half your revenue? Half your profit? (Typical answer: top 20% = 50-60% of profit.)
2. **What fraction of your customers made only one purchase last year?** Is this consistent with the 60-65% typically observed?
3. **What does the distribution of customer spend look like?** Is it right-skewed? What is the mean vs. median? What percentage of customers fall below the mean?
4. **What drives the variation in customer value -- frequency or basket size?** When you decompose profit into AOF x AOV x margin, which component varies most across deciles?
5. **What percentage of your customers were unprofitable?** What is the average loss per unprofitable customer, and what drives their negative margins (cherry-picking promotions, high return rates, etc.)?

## Lens 2: Period Comparison (Chapter 4)

*What changed since last period?*

1. **How many customers were active in both the current and prior period?** What fraction of last year's customers returned? (Typical: only 30-40% overlap between adjacent years.)
2. **Is your growth driven by customer count or customer value?** Has the average profit per active customer changed, or did you simply add more customers?
3. **What is the "same-customer" performance trend?** Among customers active in both periods, is average profitability stable, rising, or declining?
4. **Which components are driving period-over-period changes?** When you decompose the change, is it driven by AOF, AOV, margin, or customer count?
5. **How many customers dropped more than two profit deciles between periods?** Are there identifiable causes (competitive actions, pricing changes, external events)?

---

## Lens 3: Cohort Evolution (Chapter 5)

*How does customer behavior evolve over time?*

1. **What percentage of acquired customers never make a second purchase?** How does this compare to the 40-70% benchmark?
2. **How long does it take for a customer to make a second purchase?** What is the median time to second purchase? What fraction of eventual repeat buyers do so within the first 16 weeks?
3. **What does the repeat-buying trajectory look like?** Does the percentage of the cohort that is active each period decline smoothly, or are there discontinuities?
4. **What is the "value to date" (VTD) distribution of a cohort?** How much total cumulative profit has each cohort member generated? How concentrated is this across deciles?
5. **What fraction of cohort members show intermittent buying patterns?** How many are inactive in one period but return in a later period? (This challenges the "lost customer" narrative.)

---

## Lens 4: Cohort Comparison (Chapter 6)

*Are we acquiring better or worse customers over time?*

1. **Are newer cohorts developing repeat buying at the same rate as older cohorts?** When you compare the cumulative percentage making a second purchase, do newer cohorts match or lag?
2. **Do cohorts acquired in different seasons behave differently?** For example, do Q4-acquired customers have different long-term patterns than Q2-acquired customers?
3. **How do AOF, AOV, and margin trajectories compare across cohorts?** Are there systematic differences that suggest changes in customer quality?
4. **Can cross-cohort differences be linked to specific changes in the business?** Have changes in pricing, promotions, product mix, or acquisition channels affected the quality of acquired customers?
5. **Is there evidence of gradual cohort degradation?** Are newer cohorts slightly worse on key metrics? This may not be visible in aggregate data but could signal a future problem.

---

## Lens 5: Customer-Base Health (Chapter 7)

*Is our customer base getting healthier or declining?*

1. **What fraction of this year's profit comes from customers acquired this year vs. prior years?** How dependent is the firm on continuous acquisition to maintain profitability?
2. **Is the customer base growing, and if so, how?** Is growth driven by acquisition volume, improved retention of existing cohorts, or increased per-customer value?
3. **What is the annual repeat-buying rate by cohort?** How quickly does the rate stabilize after the initial shakeout? Is it stable across cohorts?
4. **How many new customers do we need to acquire next year to meet our growth targets?** Given observed repeat-buying rates and per-customer values, what does the "acquisition math" look like?
5. **Do we see any offsetting trends masked in aggregate data?** For example, increasing AOF but decreasing AOV, or improving retention but worsening acquisition quality.

## Product Dimension (Chapter 8)

*Which product categories drive profitable customer relationships?*

1. **Which categories have the highest penetration among your customers?** Are the highest-penetration categories also the highest-profit categories?
2. **What is the category-level decomposition of profit?** For each category, what is the penetration x ACOF x ACOV x avg category margin breakdown?
3. **What percentage of customers only buy from a single category?** Is this concentrated in specific categories?
4. **Which categories appear on customers' first-ever transactions?** Do entry categories predict higher or lower lifetime value?
5. **Are your highest-value customers buying more categories per transaction, or just buying more frequently?** What drives their higher AOV -- more items per category order or higher-priced items?

---

## Strategic Questions (Conclusion)

*From audit to action.*

1. **What is the expected value of a newly acquired customer?** Given observed VTD distributions and one-and-done rates, what is a realistic expectation for the average newly acquired customer?
2. **Is your acquisition spend aligned to customer value?** At the channel and campaign level, does customer acquisition cost exceed expected customer value?
3. **Which products serve as entry points for high-value customer relationships?** Should pricing or availability of these products be adjusted to attract more high-value customers?
4. **What does your "customer P&L" look like by value tier?** How much are you investing in your top customers vs. your bottom customers, and is that allocation proportional to their value?
5. **Are you acquiring customers or just transactions?** Are promotional campaigns generating genuine new customer relationships or one-time purchases by deal-seekers?

## Using These Questions

### For a First-Time Audit

Start with the foundational questions and Lens 1. If the answers reveal meaningful heterogeneity (they will), proceed through the remaining lenses in order. The full set of questions becomes a checklist for comprehensive audit coverage.

### For an Ongoing Audit

Focus on changes from the prior audit: Lens 2 for period comparison, Lens 4 for cohort comparison, and Lens 5 for overall health trends. Flag any metric that has shifted meaningfully.

### For Board or Investor Presentations

Prioritize:
- Customer concentration (Lens 1)
- Acquisition volume and quality trends (Lenses 4-5)
- Repeat-buying rates and trajectory (Lens 3)
- The "acquisition math" for next year (Lens 5)
- Any early warning signs of cohort degradation (Lens 4)

### For M&A Due Diligence

All questions apply. Pay particular attention to:
- One-and-done rates (inflated customer counts?)
- Cohort degradation trends (masking future decline?)
- Dependence on new acquisition for profit (sustainable?)
- Bottom-decile economics (hidden costs?)
