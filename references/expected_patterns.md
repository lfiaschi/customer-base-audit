# Expected Patterns Reference

What "normal" looks like in a customer-base audit, based on "The Customer-Base Audit" by Fader, Hardie & Ross.

These patterns have been observed "across hundreds of different companies" (Chapter 3). They serve as benchmarks: deviations from these patterns should be investigated and explained.

---

## 1. Right-Skewed Distributions

**Pattern:** The distributions of total spend, number of transactions, average spend per transaction, and profit per customer are almost always highly right-skewed.

**What this means:**
- The mean is higher than the median (typically: 60-70% of customers fall below the mean).
- Most customers cluster at the low end; a long tail extends to the right.
- For Madrigal: mean spend = $183, median spend = $113; 69% of customers below the mean.

**Why it matters:** Any discussion of "the average customer" is misleading. The average is pulled up by the heavy tail and does not represent the typical customer.

**Exception:** Average margin distributions tend to be relatively symmetric, with mean and median close together (e.g., 77% of Madrigal customers had margins between 40-60%).

---

## 2. One-and-Done Customers

**Pattern:** 40-70% of acquired customers never make a second purchase.

**What this means:**
- For Madrigal: 45% of the Q1/2016 cohort never made a second purchase within nearly 4 years.
- An additional 15% made a repeat purchase in the first year but never again.
- Only about 7% of a cohort makes at least one purchase every year consistently.

**Why it matters:** This is the single most surprising finding for executives who have not looked at cohort data. It profoundly shapes expected cohort economics and the importance of early repeat-purchase programs.

**Context-dependent:** Vice products (alcohol, tobacco) and online gaming tend to show more extreme skew with heavier users. Infrequent purchase categories (e.g., luxury goods, travel) will naturally show higher one-and-done rates.

---

## 3. Modal Transaction Count Is One

**Pattern:** In almost all cases, the most common number of transactions per customer in a year is one.

**What this means:**
- For Madrigal: 63% of 2019 customers made exactly one transaction; 81% made one or two.
- Just over 1% made 10+ transactions.

**Why it matters:** The majority of your customer base is extremely light. Revenue growth depends disproportionately on the minority that buy repeatedly.

---

## 4. Top-Decile Concentration

**Pattern:** The top 10% of customers typically account for 30-50% of profit.

**What this means:**
- For Madrigal: Top 10% = 40% of 2019 profit; top 20% = 58% of profit.
- When deciles are constructed by equal-profit shares, the top decile may contain only 1% of customers.

**Why it matters:** A small number of customers drive a disproportionate share of the business. Understanding who these customers are, how they behave, and where they come from is critical for growth strategy.

---

## 5. AOF as the Primary Driver

**Pattern:** Revenue variation between customers is driven more by frequency than by basket size.

**What this means:**
- AOF varies dramatically across deciles (e.g., 10.9 for decile 1 vs. 1.2 for decile 10 in Madrigal).
- AOV also varies but less dramatically (e.g., $156 for decile 1 vs. $45 for decile 10).
- Average margin shows relatively little variation across deciles (e.g., 41-50% range).
- The correlation between number of transactions and average spend per transaction is typically near zero (Madrigal: -0.03).

**Why it matters:** Strategies to increase customer value should prioritize driving frequency of purchase. Getting a customer to buy "one more time" has more impact than increasing basket size.

---

## 6. Bottom-Decile Loss Makers

**Pattern:** The bottom decile often contains customers on which the firm made a loss, or very close to zero profit.

**What this means:**
- For Madrigal: Just under 1% of 2019 customers were unprofitable (negative margin).
- Loss-making customers had AOF = 1.4, AOV = $44, and average margin = -19%.
- As cost allocation becomes more complete (adding shipping, handling, returns), the fraction of unprofitable customers grows.

**Why it matters:** Some customers cost more to serve than they generate in revenue. Identifying and managing these customers (or adjusting pricing/promotional strategies) is essential.

---

## 7. Seasonal Patterns

**Pattern:** Q4 spikes are common in retail, affecting revenue, active customer rates, and acquisition volumes.

**What this means:**
- For Madrigal: Q4 revenue and profit are typically more than double the average of the previous three quarters.
- About 45% of total customer acquisitions occur during Q4.
- The Q4 spike affects both the number of active cohort members and their average spend.
- Q4-acquired cohorts may behave differently from cohorts acquired in other quarters (higher initial activity but potentially different long-term patterns).

**Why it matters:** Seasonal effects must be accounted for when comparing periods or cohorts. Calendar-aligned and left-aligned cohort comparisons help disentangle seasonal effects from true cohort differences.

---

## 8. Cohort Attrition and Quality Improvement

**Pattern:** Activity rates decline over time, but the quality of surviving cohort members improves.

**What this means:**
- Large drop from year of acquisition to the next (e.g., only 28% of Madrigal's 2016 cohort active in 2017).
- Subsequent annual decline is more gradual (e.g., 83% of those active in 2017 are active in 2018).
- Average profit per active customer is ~30% lower in the acquisition year than in subsequent years, because:
  1. Many one-and-done buyers are in the denominator.
  2. Customers acquired late in the year have less time to make repeat purchases.
- After the "shakeout," surviving members show relatively stable behavior.
- AOF among active members tends to edge slightly upward over time.
- Repeat-buying rates increase with cohort age (e.g., from ~26% in year 1 to ~54% by year 3 for Madrigal).

**Why it matters:** The initial drop should not be alarming; it is normal. What matters is the rate and trajectory of the ongoing decline and whether cohort quality is stable, improving, or degrading over time.

---

## 9. Intermittent Buying Is Normal

**Pattern:** Lack of purchasing in one period does not mean the customer is "lost."

**What this means:**
- For Madrigal's Q1/2016 cohort: 26% had a purchasing pattern where inactivity in one year was followed by activity in a later year.
- 39% of cohort members active in 2018 were inactive in 2017.
- Only ~20% of customers active across a two-year span are active in both years.

**Why it matters:** "Win-back" campaigns targeting customers who missed one period may be premature. Terms like "retained" and "lost" should be used with extreme care in noncontractual settings. The authors recommend avoiding the word "retention" entirely in noncontractual businesses, preferring "repeat-buying rate."

---

## 10. New Customers Dominate Annual Totals

**Pattern:** A large fraction of each year's profit comes from newly acquired customers.

**What this means:**
- For Madrigal: 47-64% of annual profit came from customers acquired that year.
- Only ~53-64% of one year's profit is "retained" in the next year from existing cohorts.
- Growth is primarily driven by increasing the size of the active customer base, not by increasing the value per active customer.

**Why it matters:** Acquisition is essential for growth. Even maintaining flat revenue requires acquiring enough new customers to replace those who become inactive. Businesses must understand their "acquisition math" to set realistic targets.

---

## 11. Stable Distributions Across Periods

**Pattern:** When comparing Lens 1 distributions across two adjacent periods, the percentage distributions of spend, transactions, and profit are nearly identical.

**What this means:**
- For Madrigal: 2018 and 2019 distributions were "for all intents and purposes, the same" despite a 21% revenue increase.
- The growth came from having more customers, not from a shift in the distribution shape.

**Why it matters:** A simple Lens 1 comparison across years provides little insight. The real insights come from tracking individual customers across periods (Lens 2) and cohorts (Lenses 3-5).

---

## 12. Category Patterns

**Pattern:** High-penetration categories tend to have higher shares of sole-category buyers and lower cross-shopping with other categories.

**What this means:**
- Categories with the highest penetration drive the most profit due to volume, not margins.
- Higher-value customers buy across more categories over time, but do not buy more categories per transaction -- their higher AOF gives them more opportunities.
- Average units per category order varies more than average categories per transaction across deciles.
- Customers' set of purchased categories varies year to year, but the number of categories purchased per year is remarkably stable.
- Some lower-penetration categories may have disproportionately high ACOV (e.g., technology).

**Why it matters:** Cross-selling opportunities are real but may manifest as increased frequency rather than broader baskets. Entry categories (first-purchase categories) can predict long-term customer value.

---

## 13. Same-Customer Performance Drift

**Pattern:** Among customers active in both periods, there is typically a slight net downward drift in profitability.

**What this means:**
- For Madrigal: customers active in both 2018 and 2019 saw a net profit decline of $5.5M.
- 53% of both-period customers saw profit decrease; 47% saw it increase or stay flat.
- The "down" group has a slightly larger average loss ($162) than the "up" group's average gain ($109).

**Why it matters:** This slight erosion is normal and should be expected. It should not trigger panic or aggressive promotional interventions, which may worsen the situation. It should, however, be monitored for acceleration.
